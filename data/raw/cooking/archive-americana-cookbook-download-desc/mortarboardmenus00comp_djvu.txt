FOREWORD 

Some  of  you  may  have  -watched  Miss 
Crawford  doff  her  mortarboard  to  take  up 
a  rolling  pin  or  Miss  Sanford  hers  to  whip 
up  a  chocolate  mousse.   After  sampling  the 
results  you  have  asked:   "Please  may  I 
have  the  recipe?"  Here  are  some  of  your 
favorites,  old  and  new.   As  a  starter  we 
are  taking  the  booklet  of  recipes  collected 
by  Catherine  Parker  T43g>  Mar:ian  Shanley 
Jacobs  T44g,  and  Betty  Maury  Valentine  f44g, 
with  the  help  of  Mrs.  Linda  Brown,  and  sold 
at  the  Christmas  Bazaar  in  1942. 

While  working  on  the  Fletcher  letters 
this  summer,  Martha  von  Briesen  discovered 
the  Fletcher  familyTs  cook  books  and  those 
of  Miss  Mattie  Patteson   just  in  time  for 
some  of  their  recipes  to  be  used  in  this 
collection.   So  our  MORTARBOARD  MENUS  is 
really  an  Anniversary  Cook  Book. 

We  wish  to  acknowledge  our  appreciation 
of  the  generous  response  to  our  request  for 
recipes  and  to  express  our  regret  that  we 
lack  space  to  publish  all  that  have  come  in. 
We  have  tried  to  include  special  requests, 
foreign  dishes  and  those  that  differ  some- 
what from  the  ones  given  in  standard  cook- 
books . 

Also,  we  wish  to  thank  those  who  have 
helped  on  the  project:  Miss  Vera  Sanford, 
who  let  us  have  her  sisterfs  recipes;  Miss 
Eunice  True,  retired  head  of  the  Home 
Economics  Department  of  Berea  College,  for 
her  advice;  Frances  Bell  f55g,  Barbara 
McLamb  T55g  and  Kathleen  Peeples  T55g  for 
assistance  in  typing;  and,  of  course, 
Mr.  Penny  of  Brown-Morrison. 

We  hope  you  will  enjoy  this  phase  of 
Sweet  Briarfs  Anniversary  celebration,  the 
proceeds  of  which  will  go  to  the  Development 
Program.

BLACKBERRY  WINE 

Measure  your  berries  and  bruise  them, 
to  every  gallon  adding  a  quart  of  boil- 
ing water.   Let  the  mixture  stand  24 
hours,  stirring  occasionally.   Then 
strain  off  the  liquid  into  a  cask;  to 
every  gallon  adding  2  pounds  of  sugar. 
Cork  tight  and  let  stand  till  the  fol- 
lowing October  and  you  will  have  wine 
ready  for  use  without  further  straining 
or  boiling.   It  may  be  improved  and 
perhaps  will  keep  better  by  adding  a 
small  quantity  of  pure  French  Brandy. 

The  Fletcher  Cook  Book 


EGGNOG 

12  eggs 

16  tablespoons  sugar 
24  ounces  whiskey 
12  ounces  rum 

6  ounces  brandy* 
&   cups  milk 
4  cups  whipping  cream 

Beat  yolks  thoroughly;  add  sugar  slowly 
and  continue  to  beat.   (This  blending  of 
volks  and  sugar  is  the  "secret". )   Add 
liquor;  then  milk;  then  whipped  cream, 
and  finally  fold  in  well  beaten  whites. 
Allow  to  "age"  at  least  24  to  4^  hours, 
stirring  occasionally. 

*  Brandy  may  be  omitted  -  in  which  case 
increase  whiskey  to  27  ounces  and  rum  to 
15  ounces. 

Carol  Rice

RED  TOP  CHEESE  BALL 

$   ounce  block  processed  cheddar  cheese 

(e.g.  Old  English  or  "Vera-sharp" ) 
3  ounce  wedge  of  "blue"  cheese  or  Roquefort 
Coffee  cream,  sherry,  onion,  cayenne, 
nutmeg,  pecans 

Leave  cheese  at  room  temperature  until  soft 
enough  to  mash  with  fork.   Mix  coffee  cream 
gradually  with  cheese  mixture  until  of 
consistency  to  beat  with  electric  mixer. 
Continue  to  add  cream  gradually,  beating 
constantly,  until  smooth  and  fluffy.   Add 
|  teaspoon  raw  onion  grated  very  fine ; 
cayenne  pepper  to  taste;  dash  of  nutmeg. 
Continue  beating  at  higher  and  higher 
speed  while  adding  gradually  about  1  table- 
spoon of  sherry  wine  -  or  more  according 
to  taste.   Continue  to  beat,  adding  cream 
or  sherry  until  about  the  consistency  of 
soft  vanilla  ice  cream.   Place  in  bowl; 
cover  airtight;  keep  in  refrigerator  at 
least  six  hours  -  can  be  kept  several  days 
in  refrigerator  to  "ripen".   Just  before 
readv  to  serve,  divide  into  two  portions; 
roll  each  portion  into  ball-shape  or 
flatten  ball  into  disc-shape;  roll  ball 
in  finely  chopped  pecans,  mixed  with  finely 
chopped  parsley. 

Note:   This  last  step  must  be  quick,  as 
cheese  mixture  very  soon  becomes  too  soft 
to  handle  -  resumes  its  creamy  texture. 

Lucy  Crawford

TURKISH  SOUP 

Make  a  quart  of  soup  --  chicken  or  Iamb 
with  rice  or  vermicelli. 
Beat  2  eggs  well,  add  the  juice  of  one 
lemon  and  beat  again.   Blend  slowly  with 
soup  so  as  not  to  curdle  the  egg.   The 
best  way  to  prevent  this  is  to  remove  the 
boiling  soup  from  fire  and  then  add  the 
egg  and  lemon  mixture  slowly  or  if  serv- 
ing from  a  tureen  put  mixture  in  tureen 
and  pour  boiling  soup  over  it   and  stir 
well.   Serve  at  once. 

The  Fishers

Pour  into  a  well-greased  (Crisco  or  butter)

longer,  until  the  top  is  a  lovely  pale  gold; 
or  heat  the  oven  to  500°,  turn  off  the  heat, 
insert  the  Schaum  Torte  and  let  it  remain 
for  3  or  4  hours. 

When  ready  to  serve,  open  the  spring  form 
and  remove  the  rim  carefully,  leaving  the 
Torte  on  the  base.   Lift  the  top  crust  off 
as  carefully  as  possible  and  put  it  aside, 
temporarily.   The  Torte  may  then  be  filled 
with  whipped  cream  (this  makes  it  echtl )  or 
ice  cream,  or  even  sherbet,  and  it  may  be 
garnished  with  fresh  (or  frozen)  berries 
or  peaches.   Replace  the  top  crust  (donTt 
worry  if  it  isnTt  all  in  one  piece)  and  set 
your  masterpiece  on  the  table,  to  be  served 
before  the  admiring  eyes  of  your  guests. 
This  recipe  serves  six  comfortably,  e:ght 
in  a  pinch. 

Cautionary  hints ;   Be  sure  that  the  egg 
whites  are  at  room  temperature  and  that  the 
sugar  is  dry.   You  will  probably  have  better 
luck,  too,  if  you  bake  it  on  a  day  that 
isnTt  damp.   Schaum  Torte  may  be  made  a  day
