Market  Forms 


According  to  the  1977-78  Nationwide  Food  Consumption  Survey, 
Americans  eat  an  average  of  about  8  ounces  of  meat,  poultry, 
fish,  and/or  eggs  including  mixtures  containing  these  foods 
each  day.  A  serving  of  one  of  these  foods  is  frequently  used 
as  the  main  dish  of  a  meal.  Both  flavor  and  nutritional  contri- 
bution have  given  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs  this  prominent 
place  in  the  diet. 

Lean  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs  are  valued  as  sources  of 
protein,  vitamins  B^  and  B,_,  and  the  minerals,  iron  and  zinc. 
These  foods  also  contain  saturated  fat  and  cholesterol.  The 
calorie  content  of  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs  is  related 
to  the  amount  of  protein  and  fat  in  the  product.  Fat  has  more 
than  twice  the  calories  of  protein;  thus,  lean  pieces  of  meat, 
poultry,  or  fish  have  fewer  calories  than  the  same  size  servings 
of  meat,  poultry,  or  fish  with  more  fat. 

Variety  in  the  kinds  and  cuts  of  meat,  kinds  of  poultry  or 
fish,  and  in  the  uses  of  eggs  can  add  interest  to  daily  meals. 
Meat,  poultry,  fish,  or  eggs  can  be  used  alone  as  entrees  or 
in  soups,  salads,  casseroles,  barbecues,  and  sandwiches  of 
every  type.  The  following  sections  are  guides  for  selecting 
meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs  for  quality  and  yield;  storing 
them  safely;  and  preparing  them  flavorfully  while  maintaining 
nutrient  quality. 


When  purchasing  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs,  one  should 
take  time  to  shop  carefully,  keeping  both  yield  and  use  in 
mind.  Knowledge  of  the  various  kinds  and  cuts  of  meat,  poultry, 
and  fish  will  be  useful  in  making  selections.  Table  1,  on 
page  5  gives  numbers  of  servings  that  can  be  obtained  from 
a  pound  of  different  cuts  of  meat,  poultry,  and  fish. 

Meat. — Beef,  lamb,  pork,  and  veal  animals  have  seven  meat 
sections  that  are  closely  related  and  are  shown  in  diagram  1 
on  page  2.  The  section  from  which  a  roast,  steak,  or  chop 
is  cut  gives  a  clue  as  to  how  tender  it  will  be.  The  meat 
from  young  animals,  such  as  lamb  and  veal  calves,  is  generally 
tender.   In  more  mature  animals,  rib  and  loin  cuts  tend  to 
be  more  tender  than  leg  and  shoulder  cuts.  The  cost  per  pound 
of  tender  cuts  is  usually  higher  than  for  less  tender  cuts. 
See  page  4  for  suggestions  on  comparing  cost  per  serving.

Poultry. — Chilled  or  frozen  ready-to-cook  chickens  are  sold 
whole  and  in  halves,  quarters,  and  individual  pieces,  such 
as  breasts,  thighs,  drumsticks,  and  wings.   However,  from  the 
standpoint  of  cost,  whole  chickens  are  generally  lower  than 
individual  pieces  on  a  per  serving  basis.   Young  chickens  in- 
clude broiler-fryers,  Cornish  game  hens,  roasters,  and  capons; 
while  mature  birds  include  hens,  baking  chickens,  stewing 
chickens,  or  fowl.   Frozen  stuffed  Cornish  game  hens  or  whole 
stuffed  roasting  chickens,  ready  for  the  oven,  are  available 
in  many  markets,  but  the  price  will  be  higher  than  that  of 
a  home-stuffed  bird. 

Turkeys  marketed  as  whole  birds  are  usually  frozen.   Chilled 
turkeys  are  sometimes  available,  especially  around  Thanksgiving 
and  Christmas  holidays.   Frozen  boneless  raw  turkey  roasts 
and  boneless  cooked  turkey  rolls  are  popular  convenience  items 
and  are  generally  higher  in  cost  per  serving  than  whole  turkeys. 
In  some  supermarkets,  one  may  also  purchase  turkey  halves, 
quarters,  and  parts;  ground  raw  turkey;  and  turkey  cutlets. 

Choices  of  chilled  and  frozen  ready-to-cook  turkeys  offered 
in  the  retail  market  include  fryer-roasters  (4  to  8  pounds), 
young  hens  (8  pounds  and  up),  and  young  toms  (12  pounds  and 
up).   Frozen  stuffed  whole  turkeys  are  also  available,  but 
are  generally  more  costly  than  an  unstuffed  bird. 

Frozen  ready-to-cook  whole  ducks  weighing  3  to  7  pounds  pre- 
dominate in  the  retail  market.   Chilled  or  frozen  ready-to- 
cook  young  geese,  as  generally  available,  weigh  from  6  to  12 
pounds. 


Fish. — Seafood  from  fresh  or  salt  water  can  be  classified  as 
fin  fish  (vertebrate — with  bones  inside)  or  shellfish  (inverte- 
brate— with  shell  on  outside).   Fin  fish  can  be  fat,  lean, 
or  intermediate  in  fat  content.   Commonly  known  lean  fish  (2 
percent  or  less  fat)  include  grouper,  Alaskan  pollock,  Pacific 


ocean  perch,  Atlantic  ocean  perch,  rockflsh,  haddock,  cod, 
sole,  flounder.  Pacific  halibut,  and  red  snapper.  Fish  inter- 
mediate in  fat  content  (2  to  6  percent  fat)  include  brook  trout, 
whiting,  catfish.  Gulf  butterfish,  giant  sea  and  striped  bass, 
chum  and  pink  salmon,  buffalo  fish,  swordfish,  carp,  and  black 
and  sea  bass.  Fish  with  a  higher  fat  content  (6  to  20  percent 
fat)  include  rainbow  trout,  whitefish,  pompano,  Atlantic  salmon, 
American  eel,  lake  (siscewet)  trout,  Atlantic  king  and  Spanish 
mackerel,  and  red  sockeye,  king,  and  Chinook  salmon. 

Fin  fish  are  marketed  fresh  or  frozen,  whole  or  dressed,  or 
as  steaks,  fillets,  and  chunks;  frozen  portions  and  sticks 
of  finfish  are  also  readily  available.  Shellfish  are  marketed 
fresh  or  frozen,  cooked  or  uncooked. 

Eggs. — Eggs  are  available  in  several  sizes:  Extra  large,  large, 

medium,  and  small.  These  sizes  are  based  on  the  minimum  weight 

per  dozen.  Large  eggs,  the  size  on  which  most  recipes  are 

based,  must  weigh  at  least  24  ounces  per  dozen.

Inspection. — The  Federal  inspection  stamp  (see  illustration) 
on  meat,  poultry,  or  fish  tells  consumers  that  the  product 
came  from  a  healthy  animal,  that  it  was  processed  under  sani- 
tary conditions,  and  that  it  is  accurately  and  informatively 
labeled.

All  fresh  and  processed  meat  and  poultry  products  that  are 
shipped  from  one  State  to  another  must  bear  a  Federal  inspec- 
tion stamp.  This  mark  assures  consumers  that  the  products 
have  either  been  inspected  by  the  Federal  Government  or  by 
an  adequate  State  system. 

Grade. — Unlike  inspection,  government  grading  is  optional; 
thus,  not  all  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs  are  federally 
graded.  Grading  is  paid  for  by  the  packer. 

Generally,  the  grade  of  meat,  poultry,  and  fish  is  based  on 
color  and  texture,  and  on  the  proportion  and  distribution  of 
flesh,  fat,  and  bone.  Poultry  is  rated  for  freedom  from  defor- 
mity, pinfeathers,  bruises,  and  skin  breaks.  Egg  grade  is 
based  on  thickness  of  whites,  firmness  of  yolks,  and  cleanliness 
of  unbroken  shells.  Poultry  and  fish  grades  are  "A,"  "B," 
and  "C;"  egg  grades  are  "AA,"  "A,"  and  "B." 

The  grades  of  beef,  lamb,  and  veal  generally  sold  in  retail 
stores  are  "Prime,"  "Choice,"  and  "Good."  "Prime"  and  "Choice" 
grade  cuts  are  usually  more  tender  and  juicy  than  a  "Good" 
grade  of  the  same  cut;  however,  meat  graded  "Good"  can  be  made 
tender  by  moist-heat  cooking.  Pork  grades  are  based  primarily 
on  proportion  of  lean  to  fat  and  are  seldom  used  for  retail  cuts. 


The  grade  stamp  is  a  colored  shield  enclosing  the  grade 
designation  with  the  letters  "USDA"  for  meat,  poultry,  and 
eggs  and  "U.S."  for  fish. 


Yield 


To  economize  when  buying  meat,  poultry,  or  fish,  buy  only  the 
quantity  that  can  be  used.   One  can  learn  to  estimate  the 
approximate  number  of  servings  per  pound  of  meat,  poultry, 
and  fish  while  making  selections  at  the  meat  counter.   These 
simple  suggestions  are  useful: 


Items  with  little  or  no  fat  or  bone,  such  as  boneless 
meat  cuts  and  fish  fillets,  will  yield  three  to  four 
3-ounce  servings  per  pound. 

Items  with  much  bone,  gristle,  or  fat,  such  as  spareribs 
and  poultry  wings  and  backs,  will  yield  one  to  two  3- 
ounce  servings  per  pound. 

Table  1  on  pages  5  and  6  shows  the  amount  to  buy  for  many  kinds 
and  cuts  of  meat,  poultry,  and  fish.   Using  the  table,  one 
will  find  that  a  boneless  beef  round  roast  is  a  better  buy 
than  a  bone-in  chuck  roast  if  price  comparisons  indicate  that 
the  price  per  serving  is  less  for  the  round  roast  than  for 
the  chuck  roast. 


STORAGE 


Fresh  meat,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs. — These  products  should 
be  stored  at  refrigerator  temperatures  of  40  F  or  below. 
For  details,  see  table  2  on  page  7.  Fresh  meats  and  fish 
generally  keep  best  if  they  are  loosely  wrapped.  However, 
the  transparent  wrap  on  prepackaged  meat,  poultry,  and  fish 
is  designed  for  refrigerator  storage  at  home  for  1  to  2  days. 
Meat  or  poultry  purchased  in  market  paper  should  be  placed 
on  a  platter  or  a  tray,  covered  with  wax  paper,  and  refriger- 
ated.  Fish  should  be  wrapped  airtight.  Eggs  should  be  stored 
in  the  carton,  large  end  up. 

Meat,  poultry,  and  fish. — These  items  may  be  purchased  frozen, 
but  most  are  frozen  at  home.  Fresh  meat,  poultry,  and  fish 
that  will  not  be  cooked  within  recommended  refrigerator  storage 
times  should  be  frozen.   Table  2  on  page  7  lists  the  length 
of  time  these  items  can  be  stored.  Store  at  0  F  or  below 
in  the  freezer  or  in  separate  freezer  section  of  refrigerator- 
freezer  where  temperature  can  be  maintained  at  3  F.   The 
frozen  food  compartment  inside  a  home  refrigerator  is  not 
designed  to  maintain  0  F.

Meat,  poultry,  and  fish  products. — These  should  be  stored  in 
the  refrigerator  in  their  original  wrappings,  or  be  loosely 
wrapped  in  other  material.  Freezer  storage  is  not  recommended 
for  some  cured  products;  only  short  freezer  storage  times  are 
suggested  for  others  since  the  salt  used  in  the  curing  process 
accelerates  flavor  changes  like  rancidity.  For  storage  times, 
see  table  2  on  page  7. 

Meats,  poultry,  fish,  and  eggs. — These  foods,  and  main  dishes 
prepared  with  them,  should  be  covered  or  wrapped  and  stored 
in  the  refrigerator  or  freezer  as  quickly  as  possible.  Gravy, 
broth,  and  poultry  stuffing  should  be  stored  in  separate 
containers  from  the  meat  product. 

Table  2  on  page  7  gives  suggested  home  refrigerator  and 
freezer  storage  times  for  best  eating  quality  for  cooked  items. 
If  it  is  anticipated  that  a  product  will  be  kept  longer  than 
the  suggested  refrigerator  storage  time,  plan  to  freeze  the 
food  in  a  moisture-vapor-proof  wrapping  or  container.

Thawing  Meat, 
Poultry,  and  Fish 


Most  meat,  poultry,  and  fish  can  be  cooked  without  thawing; 
however,  if  thawing  is  desired,  plan  so  that  thawing  is  com- 
pleted just  before  cooking.  The  item  should  be  thawed  in 
the  refrigerator.   Do  not  thaw  commercially  frozen  stuffed 
poultry  before  cooking.   Other  prepared  items  such  as  meat 
patties,  breaded  fish,  and  fried  chicken  should  also  be  cooked 
without  thawing.   Check  the  label  of  prepared  foods  before 
thawing.  For  thawing  times,  see  table  3  on  page  8.

Broiling 

Broiling,  cooking  by  direct  heat  from  a  flame,  electric  unit, 
or  glowing  coals,  is  a  suitable  cooking  method  for  tender 
cuts  of  meat,  small  young  chicken  halves  or  pieces,  small 
fryer-roaster  turkey  pieces,  fish  fillets  and  steaks,  and 
some  shellfish.  For  broiling  times,  see  table  4  on  page  13. 

To  broil,  place  the  meat,  poultry,  or  fish  on  the  slotted 
grid  or  rack  on  the  broiler  pan.   If  needed,  snap  joints 
of  poultry  pieces  to  flatten.  Turn  only  once  during  cooking. 
Well-done  steaks  and  chops,  fish  (cooked  only  on  one  side 
to  prevent  breaking),  chicken  halves,  and  turkey  pieces  are 
cooked  farther  from  the  heat  source  than  are  rare  steaks. 
For  broiling  times,  see  table  4.  Follow  broiler  instructions 
for  regulation  of  heat  during  broiling.  Broil  meat  until  cooked 
medium  to  well-done.  Beef  steaks  may  also  be  cooked  to  the 
rare  stage.  Broil  poultry  and  fish  until  well-done.   Using 
broiling  times  in  table  4  as  a  guide,  determine  doneness  by 
color  in  meat.  Well-done  meat  is  light  brown  throughout  with 
clear  juices,  while  medium-done  meat  has  a  light  pink  interior. 
Poultry  is  done  when  it  is  fork  tender  and  the  juices  are  clear; 
fish  is  done  when  it  flakes  easily  with  a  fork. 


Roasting 

Roasting,  or  cooking  uncovered,  is  generally  done  in  a  con- 
ventional oven  and  is  suitable  for  tender  meat  roasts,  whole 
chickens  and  turkeys,  large  pieces  of  chicken  and  turkey, 
and  whole  fish.   Liquid  or  water  is  not  added.   For  roasting 
times,  see  table  5  on  pages  13  to  15. 

To  roast,  place  food  on  a  rack  in  a  shallow  pan  and  roast  to 
the  desired  degree  of  doneness.  Use  table  5  as  a  guide  to 
roasting  time.   Internal  temperatures  indicating  meat  or  poultry 
doneness  are  also  given  in  the  table.   Use  the  following  checks 
for  doneness: 

.  Determine  the  internal  temperature  of  the  meat  or  poultry  by 
inserting  a  meat  thermometer  in  the  center  of  the  thickest 
muscle  of  meat  and  in  the  center  of  the  inner  thigh  of  large 
turkeys.   The  internal  temperatures  shown  in  the  timetable 
(table  5)  will  indicate  that  the  desired  degree  of  doneness 
has  been  reached. 

.  Check  the  color  of  the  meat.  Well-done  meat  will  be  light 
brown  throughout  with  no  pink  color  evident.  The  juice 
will  be  clear.  Medium-done  meat  will  be  light  pink  in  the 
center. 

.  Poultry  is  done  when  the  leg  joints  move  easily,  the 
thigh  meat  is  soft  when  pressed  with  the  finger,  and 
the  juices  run  clear. 

.  Fish  is  done  when  it  flakes  easily  with  a  fork. 

Remove  roasts,  poultry,  or  fish  from  the  oven  when  done.  Let 
large  roasts  or  poultry  stand  15  to  20  minutes  to  make  slicing 
easier. 

Braising 

Braising,  or  cooking  in  steam  in  a  covered  container  or  foil 
wrap,  is  a  popular  method  for  tenderizing  mature  chicken  and  less 
tender  meat  cuts.  The  source  of  steam  may  be  water  or  other 
liquid  added  to  the  meat  or  poultry,  or  it  may  be  meat  or 
poultry  juices  alone.   Fish  has  very  little  connective  tissue; 
thus,  it  is  usually  not  braised. 

For  additional  flavor,  brown  meat  cuts  before  braising.  To 
braise,  place  meat  or  poultry  in  pan,  cover,  and  cook  until 
tender.  Then  remove  cover  and  allow  poultry  skin  to  crisp  the 
last  few  minutes  of  cooking,  if  desired.   Use  table  6  on  pages 
16  to  17  as  a  time  guide  for  braising  the  specific  cuts  recom- 
mended for  this  method. 


Simmering 

Simmering  means  cooking  in  liquid  that  is  kept  just  below  the 
boiling  point.   Cubes  of  meat  for  stew,  whole  hams,  corned 
beef,  and  mature  chickens  are  tenderized  by  simmering.   To 
simmer,  add  water  to  barely  cover  meat,  poultry,  fish,  or  eggs 
(in  or  out  of  the  shell);  season  as  desired;  and  cover.   Cook 
slowly  over  low  heat.  Brown  meat  (except  ham  and  corned  beef) 
before  cooking,  if  desired.   For  simmering  times  on  recommended 
cuts,  see  table  7  on  page  18. 

Fish. — Use  a  wire  basket  or  a  piece  of  foil  or  parchment  with 
edges  turned  up  to  hold  fish  and  to  prevent  it  from  breaking. 
Simmer  fish  only  until  it  flakes  easily  with  a  fork. 

Eggs. — Remove  poached  eggs  (eggs  cooked  out  of  the  shell)  from 
the  water  in  which  they  were  simmered  as  soon  as  they  are  done, 
to  prevent  overcooking.   Plunge  hard-cooked  eggs  into  cold 
water  after  cooking  for  easier  peeling. 

Cooking  Frozen      Extended  Cooking  Time. — If  frozen  meat  is  not  thawed  before 
Meat,  Poultry,      cooking,  cooking  time  will  generally  need  to  be  extended, 
and  Fish  The  amount  of  extra  time  depends  on  the  size,  shape,  and  thick-

Frozen  large  meat  roasts,  frozen  steaks,  and  frozen  chops  may
