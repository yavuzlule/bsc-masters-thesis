HOMEWOOD  TERRACE 
DEVELOPMENT  UNDERWAY 


This  recent  OMI  News  photo  depicts  preliminary  work  on 
the  new  project. 

Community  residents  have  doubtless  noticed  the  recent 
construction  activity  in  Homewood  Terrace.  For  the  past  six 
weeks,  demolition  crews  have  been  at  work  razing  the  old 
■walls  and  buildings  remaining  irom  the  site’s  former  use  as 
the  Jewish  Home  Association.  That  portion  of  the  project 
has  now  been  completed. 

Mark  Rafanelli,  Project  Supervisor  for  Doric- 
Development,  disclosed  his  firm’s  plans  for  the  property  in 
a  recent  telephone  interview  with  the  OMI  News.  For  those 
who  may  not  be  familiar  with  local  geography,  the  first  300 
feet  of  the  property’s  depth  are  zoned  for  commercial  use 

Doric’s  proposal  for  that  part  of  the  development  involves 
the  construction  of  small,  village-type  shops  and,  hopefully, 
of  a  chain  grocery  store.  We  understand  that  title  to  all  of 
the  residential  component  and  to  approximately  half  of  the 
commercial  one  has  been  perfected.  Mr.  Rafanelli  indicated 
that  the  purchase  of  the  remaining  commercial  property  is 
presently  being  negotiated  with  its  present  owner,  Pacific 
Telephone. 

The  News  was  told  that  all  relevant  building  permits  and 
environmental  impact  reports  have  been  approved.  The 
principal  remaining  difficulty  appears  to  be  in  the  area  of 
securing  long-term  financing  for  the  project  because  of 
today’s  uncertain  capital  market. 

Rafanelli  indicated  that  he  would  keep  OMI-CA  abreast  of 
the  project’s  development.

To  The  People 

By  ROY  D.  JACKSON 

Now  that  the  holidays  are  approaching,  we  should  be  very 
happy  and  prepare  for  the  enjoyable  time  that  all  are 
expecting.  Therefore,  I  won’t  try  and  say  anything  to  in  any 
way  change  this  anticipated  joyous  time  of  our  lives. 

However,  there  is  the  need  to  remind  all  of  you  that 
without  continuing  efforts  on  our  part  we  will  not  be  able  to 
do  the  things  that  a  community  organization  such  as  ours 
would  like  to  do.  Services  are  needed  in  our  community  for 
the  many  senior  citizens  and  our  ever-growing  population  of 
teenagers,  who  demand  recognition  by  their  very  existence. 
We  need  to  implement  a  program  to  give  hopes  to  this  new 
generation  while  respecting  their  views  and  giving  them 
direction  in  their  plans. 

In  the  near  future  we  hope  to  announce  some  good  news 
that  will,  we  hope,  arouse  each  resident  of  the  Ocean 
View-Merced  Heights-Ingleside  district  to  rally  to  the 
support  of  OMI-CA.  Our  aim  is  to  make  our  community  one 
that  is  envied  by  all  other  communities.  I  do  hope  that  you 
will  let  our  office,  your  Community  Organization,  know  your 
feeling  on  projects  we  undertake  to  improve  the  community. 

As  the  Association’s  President,  I  would  like  to  say  that  I 
have  enjoyed  a  very  pleasant  two-year  term .  I  have  tried  to 
serve  in  this  capacity  to  the  best  of  my  ability.  I  have 
encountered  some  fine  and  dedicated  people  and  hope  that 
the  future  will  bring  total  community  involvement  in  the 
development  and  implementation  of  many  new  programs.  I 
believe  that  we  can  look  forward  to  some  new  and 
innovative  ventures  once  the  political  scene  settles.  Please 
remember  that  your  input  is  invaluable  to  us 

Peace  and  love  to  all!

CONSUMER  LEGISLATION 

Californians,  watching  cash  registers  ring  up  higher  and 
higher  prices  during  the  past  year,  know  that  the  consumer 
dollar  doesn’t  stretch  as  far  as  it  once  did. 

This  session  the  State  Legislature  acted  on  a  number  of 
bills  to  save  consumers  money  and  to  better  define 
people’s  rights  as  tenants  and  purchasers  of  goods  and 
services. 

Governor  Brown  signed  legislation  in  three  important 
consumer  areas:  fair  trade  laws,  generic  drug  substitution, 
and  retail  price  marking. 

Assembly  Bill  1109,  authored  by  Assemblyman  Allister 
McAlister,  repeals  the  State’s  Fair  Trade  laws  which  allow 
manufacturers  to  set  inflexible  retail  prices  for  certain 
brands  of  appliances,  toys,  audio  equipment  and  cooking 
ware. 

Consumer  advocates  say  that  the  laws  —  adopted  more 
than  four  decades  ago  to  aid  small  businessmen  —  lawfully 
sanctioned  price  fixing.  Retailers  who  wished  to  price 
fair-traded  goods  more  competitively  could  be  prosecuted 
under  the  old  law  for  selling  at  less  than  the  manufacturer’s 
list  price. 

Most  Californians  will  benefit  from  the  lowered  prices.  It 
has  been  estimated  that  the  average  family  in  the  State  will 
save  about  $120  a  year  when  the  repeal  goes  into  effect 
January  1. 

A  second  new  consumer  law,  AB  193,  sponsored  by 
Assemblyman  Barry  Keene  authorizes  a  phaynacist  to 
substitute  a  less  expensive  chemically  equivalent  drug  for  a 
drug  prescribed  by  a  brand  or  trade  name. 

Physicians  may  check  off  a  box  indicating  “no 
substitutions”  on  prescription  forms.  The  Director  of  the 
Department  of  Health  will  also  establish  a  list  of  drugs 
which  should  not  be  substituted.

I  was  delighted  to  get  my  hands  on  the  Ballantine  printing 
of  THE  YEARS  WITH  ROSS  by  James  Thurber.  I  read  it 
when  it  first  appeared  in  hardcover  back  in  1957  and  if  it’s  at 
all  possible,  I  love  it  even  more  on  this  second  reading.  Who 
was  Ross?  Merely  the  founder  and  guiding  genius  of  The 
New  Yorker,  who  ran  it  lovingly  for  over  a  quarter  of  a 
century  through  Boom,  Bust  and  Wartime.  Thurber 
recreates  those  exciting  years  with  wit  and  the  accumulated 
wisdom  of  one  who  worked  intimately  with  the  editor  for  all 
but  two  of  those  years.  This  is  a  wonderfully  written  book, 
warm  as  well  as  humorous. 

One  of  the  more  exciting  action  books  to  reach  my  desk  is 
Avon  s  THE  EIGER  SANCTION.  Its  author,  who  prefers  to 
be  known  by  just  his  last  name,  Trevanian,  uses 
considerable  skill  in  keeping  the  reader  guessing  as  to  who 
was  responsible  for  what  and  who  did  what  to  whom. 
“Eiger”  refers  to  a  mountain  in  the  Alps,  and  “Sanction”  is 
the  euphemistic  term  for  assassination  or  killing.  Trevanian 
has  a  sense  of  humor,  too,  and  employs  it  with  his  choice  of 
names.  For  example,  the  head  of  the  bureu  hiring  Jonathan 
Hemlock  for  the  sanction  is  called  Yurassis  Dragon.  Happy 
climbing  and  don’t  forget  your  Alpenstock! 

To  tell  the  truth  (as  I  strive  to  do  on  occasion),  I’d 
forgotten  that  Bantam’s  THE  DAY  OF  THE  LOCUST  by 
Nathanael  West  was  in  the  house.  But  having  gotten  a 
chance  to  see  the  film  when  it  was  at  the  Bridge  Theatre, 
and  read  some  mixed  reviews  of  it,  I  thought  I’d  re-read  it  to 
find  what  changes  were  made  from  the  book.  A  number 
were  made,  to  be  sure,  and  I  can’t  say  they  were  for  the 
better.  If  you  see  the  film  first  (please  do  so)  it’s  an 
intriguing  one  even  with  its  flaws.  However,  if  you  forego 
the  film  entirely,  or  see  it  first,  DON’T  NEGLECT  THE 
BOOK!  It’s  a  penetrating  glimpse  into  Hollywood  that  was 
and  a  time  when,  to  paraphrase  Napoleon,  “every  child 
carried  a  passport  to  stardom  with  his  or  her  tap  dancing 
shoes.”  (O.K.,  O.K.  The  actual  Napoleonic  quote  was  that

one.  Here’s  a  simple  recipe  for  ground  meat  that’s  a  sure 
winner  and  good  enough  for  guests. 

l'/2-2  lbs.  course  ground  chuck,  round  or  sirloin

meat  and  serve  immediately.  Serve  on  checkered  red  table 
cloth  with  hot  French  bread,  salad,  wine  or  ale.  This  is

Under  the  third  major  consumer  law,  Senate  Bill  261, 
authored  by  Senator  David  Roberti,  stores  will  be  required 
to  label  individual  goods  with  prices. 

The  bill  was  passed  in  response  to  consumer  concern 
about  the  new  computerized  checkout  stands  which  are 
being  tested  in  some  of  the  State’s  supermarkets. 

The  computer  totals  a  shopper’s  bill  by  reading  coded 
price,  involving  a  series  of  black  lines,  on  each  item.  Many 
retailers  installing  the  new  system  have  not  been  marking 
each  product  with  a  number  people  can  read.  Prices  are 
listed  on  the  shelves  only. 

Consumer  groups  feel  items  should  be  marked  separately 
enabling  shoppers  to  compare  prices  on  items  located  in 
various  parts  of  the  store  and  to  allow  them  to  compare  the 
marked  price  with  the  price  the  computer  rings  up.
