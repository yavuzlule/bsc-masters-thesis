More  than  100  parents,  teach¬ 
ers,  students  and  community 
people  carrie  out  on  May  18  for  the 
purpose  of  establishing  goals  and 
priorities  for  Aptos  Junior  High. 

Those  at  the  conference  sought 
to  reach  a  consensus  in  2 
important  areas:  What  are  the 
things  that  are  keeping  Aptos 
from  doing  the  job  it  should  do  for 
the  students?”  and 

What  should  Aptos  be  doing  for 
the  students?  When  students 
graduate  from  the  school:  What 
knowledge  should  they  have? 
What  skills  should  they  have? 
What  kinds  of  attitudes  should 
they  have? 

Each  and  every  San  Francisco 
school  has  held  or  will  hold  a 
community  conference  of  this 
kind.  Vasconciellos’  legislation, 
known  as  “Education  for  the 
People”  makes  mandatory  these 
efforts  to  involve  the  school 
community  in  the  formulation  of 
educational  goals  and  priorities. 


"Education  For  The  People" 


In  the  foreground  facing  the  camera  is  Vernon  Wallace,  incoming 
president  of  Aptos  PTA.  At  the  conference,  each  group  writes  down  its 
ideas  and  conclusions,  then  passes  them  to  another  group  which  may 
make  changes  or  additions  before  passing  them  on  again.  By  this 
method  a  lot  of  ideas  can  be  considered  in  much  less  time  than  in  a 
conventional  meeting.

church  a  little  after  eight  crossed 
the  overpass,  quickly  followd  by

Dialogue 

With  Assemblyman  Leo  McCarthy 

May  I  remind  all  senior  citizen  homeowners  of  the  OMI  to  apply  for 
the  State  Senior  Citizen  Property  Tax  Assistance  program? 

This  little-publicized  program  is  designed  to  assist  all  senior  citizens 
who  own  their  own  home  by  reimbursing  them  a  portion  of  their 
property  tax  payments.  The  Property  Tax  Assistance  Program  is  the 
result  of  the  1972  legislative  tax  reform  package  designed  to  take  the 
burden  off  the  beleaguered  property  taxpayer,  and  especially  the 
elderly  homeowner  on  a  fixed  income. 

You  will  not  receive  an  official  notice  from  the  State  Franchise  Tax 
Board  announcing  your  eligibility.  It  is  up  to  you  to  apply  for  the 
property  tax  reimbursement.  Please  do  not  confuse  this  State 
program  with  the  $1750  City  Homeowners  Property  Tax  Exemption 
program,  for  which  I  hope  you  have  already  filed:  These  are  two 
entirely  different  programs! 

Since  the  refund  is  substantial  for  most  senior  citizen  homeowners 
on  fixed  incomes,  I  urge  you  to  apply  even  if  you  believe  you  may  not 
qualify. 

To  Qualify:  1.  Person  must  be  62  years  of  age  on  or  before  January 
1,1974. 

2.  Be  a  resident  of  California  on  or  before  Januarv  1 
1974. 

3.  Own  and  occupy  the  home  on  which  assistance  is 
claimed. 

4.  Have  a  yearly  household  income  of  $10,000  or  less. 
(All  income  must  be  reported  including  Social 
Security  and  Social  Services  payments.) 

5.  Beginning  this  year,  elderly  homeowners  receiving 
public  assistance  are  also  eligible  for  property  tax 
assistance. 

To  Apply :  Write  for  application  forms  between  May  15,  1974  and 

August  31,  1974  to: 

Franchise  Tax  Board, 

State  Building,  345  Larkin  Street 
San  Francisco,  California  94102. 

Telephone:  557-0540  for  information  or  557-2357  for  application 

forms  only. 

Or  call  my  office  for  assistance  at  557-2662.

TO  THE  PEOPLE 


Board  Briefs 

By  GENEVIEVE  JEFFERSON,  Secretary  of  OMI-CA 

The  Board  of  Directors  took  action  ordering  a  letter  written  to 
Mayor  Alioto  with  copies  to  the  Police  Commission,  Chief  of  Police, 
and  the  Civil  Service  Commission,  urging  that  action  be  taken  to 
schedule  civil  service  examinations  for  policemen  which  would  make 
possible  more  policemen  on  the  streets.  A  number  of  minority 
candidates  have  taken  training  for  civil  service  examinations,  and  are 
ready  to  take  the  examination  for  policeman  when  one  is  scheduled. 
There  has  been  no  examination  in  approximately  two  years. 

Representatives  from  the  Parents  Club  at  Sloat  appeared  before  the 
Board  telling  of  their  efforts  to  have  that  school  set  up  as  an  Early 
Childhood  Education  School  and  asking  the  support  of  the  OMI 
Community  Association.  There  are  10  such  schools  in  San  Francisco 
but  none  in  the  OMI  area.  Because  this  was  a  complicated  and 
somewhat  controversial  matter  the  Board  was  reluctant  to  act  without 
further  information.  They  referred  the  matter  to  the  Education 
Committee  for  evaluation.  That  Committee,  after  studying  the  matter, 
reported  back  recommending  that  the  Board  approve  this  program 
with  the  provision  that  a  Parent  Education  Program  be  included  and 
this  was  done. 

There  has  been  a  good  deal  of  interest  in  development  of  a  Youth 
Center  in  the  OMI  area.  However,  the  Board  was  reminded  that  the 
Ocean  View  gym  is  outstanding  in  Northern  California,  has  excellent 
staff  and  equipment,  yet  is  not  being  utilized  fully.  It  was  agreed  that 
steps  should  be  taken  to  work  with  the  Recreation  Park  Department 
staff  and  if  necessary  to  secure  added  police  coverage  to  make  this 
area  more  desirable  rather  than  to  seek  another  site. 

The  President  of  OMI-CA  has  been  meeting  with  representatives  of 
the  Mayor  in  the  effort  to  develop  jobs  for  youth  in  this  area.  Last  year 
there  were  200  such  jobs.  This  year’s  plans  are  not  yet  finalized.  There 
is  need  for  a  job  site  and  a  supervisor,  preferably  a  college  student. 
More  information  should  be  available  in  about  two  weeks. 

Mrs.  Avis  Bateman,  a  Quality  Reviewer  with  the  Social  Security 
Administration  is  the  most  recent  Board  member.  She  has  previously 
been  active  in  the  community  and  now  brings  her  many  talents  to 
serve  on  the  Board  of  Directors.

BETHEL  LUTHERAN 
AIDS  RETARDED  ADULTS 

By  KENNETH  BAUER,  Pastor, 
Bethel  Lutheran  Church 

Three  years  ago,  we  “dis¬ 
covered”  that  in  the  Outer 
Mission  and  Ingleside  District  of 
San  Francisco  there  is  a  dispro¬ 
portionately  large  number  of 
mentally  retarded  resident 
adults.  The  people  of  Bethel 
Lutheran  Church,  2525  Alemany 
Blvd.,  felt  that  they  not  only  had 
the  resources  but  the  will  to 
provide  some  help  for  these 
people. 

We  began  with  a  Sunday  School 
program  which  soon  grew  to 
numbers  greater  than  could  be 
handled.  We  decided  to  begin  a 
conventional  day  care  center  — 
but  with  some  unique  features. 

The  emphasis  at  the  Bethel 
Center  for  the  Developmentaliy 
Disabled  is  to  provide  a  place 
where  each  person  is  accepted 
and  can  develop  not  only  his  skills 
but  his  individual  identity.  Basics 
such  as  reading,  counting,  telling 
time,  learning  money  exchanges, 
etc.,  are  taught  to  those  capable. 
Handcrafts  are  shown  for  plea¬ 
sure  as  well  as  development  of 
coordination  and  occupational 
skills.  Bethel’s  mini -gym  serves 
for  leisure  recreation  plus  de¬ 
velopment  of  stamina  and  physi¬ 
cal  adeptness. 

The  social  life  of  the  students  is 
important  and  a  sense  of  family 
at  the  school  helps  them  to  feel 
needed  and  wanted.  We  also  aim 
to  help  this  “family”  learn  to 
function  better  within  the  com¬ 
munity,  making  use  of  services 
available  through  stores,  public 


transportation,  entertainment, 
etc. 

The  first  distinctive  feature  of 
the  school  is  our  commitment  to 
the  religious  education  of  these 
students. 

Another  unique  aspect  of  the 
Bethel  Center  is  that  the  school 
has  a  living  involvement  in  the 
life  of  the  congregation.  Numer¬ 
ous  people  of  the  congregation 
come  to  share  a  skill,  a  hobby,  a 
pet,  etc.;  the  class  participates  in 
Christmas  programs,  picnics  and 
other  congregational  functions. 
Our  church  belongs  to  them,  and 
the  members  become  a  part  of 
their  larger  family.  This  of 
course  opens  a  new  world  of 


possibilities  for  growth  on  both 
sides. 

In  the  future  we  hope  to 
investigate  a  program  whereby 
employers  may  contract  with  the 
Center  to  employ  our  students,  to 
provide  spending  money  for 
them.  We  are  excited  about  this 
prospect  and  anxious  to  see  how 
we  will  grow  with  it. 

One  of  our  immediate  goals  is 
to  expand  Bethel  Center’s  pro¬ 
gram  by  taking  in  new  students 
so  that  by  early  1975  we  might 
have  2  programs  in  operation, 
each  with  from  8  to  10  students. 

The  congregation,  through  the 
support  of  the  Lutheran  Church, 
has  both  the  desire  and  the  means 
to  expand,  if  the  need  is  clearly 
seen.  We  encourage  interested 
people  to  either  visit  the  Center 
or  to  call  for  further  information. 
Contact  Mrs.  Melda  Brown,  the 
coordinator-teacher,  at  755-0253 
or  994-0869,  or  Rev.  Kenneth 
Bauer,  587-2525.

FIND  A  POT  AND  FILL  H 

By  JOE  MANNON 

During  the  long  hot  OMI  summers,  as  the  warm 
breezes  from  the  Japanese  Current  waft  gently 
over  our  sun  kissed  hills,  a  rich  and  simply 
prepared  dish  from  the  Levant,  served  with  a 
favorite  green  or  fresh  fruit  salad,  eases  the 
burden  of  the  day,  and  colors  our  life  with  the  idyllic  image 
peaceful  Mediterranean  existence,  ow  much  more  poetic  and 
palatable  this  wishful  description  than  ...  “As  the  westerly  wind  from 
the  Japanese  Current  wraps  our  hillsides  in  a  deathly  damp  grey 
shroud.”  However,  what  we  lack  in  sunshine  during  the  summer  we 
make  up  for  with  an  equally  bright  disposition  generated,  no  doubt,  by 
the  fact  that  while  outlying  areas  are  sweltering  in  the  heat  and  smog 
we  can  escape  into  our  gray  refuge.  This  months  offering  from  Mary 
Assily  is  equally  at  home  in  either  climate,  although  it  does  have 
Middle  East  origins. 


UPSIDE  DOWN  MEAT  AND  RICE 

1  eggplant,  peeled  and  sliced  V2”  thick,  OR  1  cauliflower 

broken  into  pieces 

1%  lbs.  ground  beef  or  lamb 

2  tsp.  salt 

Me  tsp  pepper 

‘4  tsp.  allspice 

1  clove  garlic,  minced 

Me  cup  chopped  onion 

2  cups  long-grain  rice,  soaked  10  min.  in  water 

Fry  vegetables  in  hot  oil  until  golden  brown;  drain  on  paper  towels. 
Pour  off  excess  oil  and  brown  meat  and  onions.  Add  seasonings  and 
garlic.  In  a  large,  heavy  pot,  place  the  meat  and  onions  on  bottom; 
next,  layer  the  vegetable  and  then  add  the  soaked,  drained  rice.  Slowly 
add  4  cups  boiling  water;  do  not  stir., Bring  to  boil,  cover,  and  simmer 
until  water  is  absorbed.  Turn  off  heat  and  let  stand,  covered,  for  15 
minutes.  Invert  on  a  round  platter.  Let  stand  10  minutes  more. 
Remove  pan  slowly  so  that  rice  does  not  lose  shape  of  pan.  Garnish 
with  parsley.  Serve  with  red  or  chilled  white  wine. 


When  1  tried  this  recipe,  I  just  lined  the  pan  with  the  eggplant  skin, 
purple  side  down,  for  added  color.

Many  Services  For 
Senior  Citizens 

By  BILL  FULLER 
Mr.  Jack  McKay  from  the  San 
Francisco  Council  of  Churches 
was  the  guest  speaker  at  the  May 
meeting  of  the  OMI  Ecumenical 
Parish. 

The  Council  of  Churches  has  for 
the  past  two  years  been  awarded 
the  contract  for  the  Geriatric 
Services  program  of  District  V 
Mental  Health.  This  program 
encompasses  the  OMI  area.  Jack 
is  in  charge  of  the  program.  He 
was  accompanied  by  two  of  his 
staff,  David  Cross  and  Mary 
Alice  Stevenson. 

Jack  pointed  out  that  San 
Francisco  has  a  high  proportion 
of  senior  citizens;  the  Geriatric 
Services  contract  provides  for 
three  distinct  programs  for  the 
elderly. 

First,  is  the  home  evaluation 
team.  This  team  is  composed  of 
three  professional  people  —  a 
social  worker,  a  nurse  and  a 
psychiatrist.  Their  purpose  is  to 
go  into  the  homes  of  elderly 
people  who  are  having  problems 
and  to  mobilize  the  services  of  the 
community  to  help  them  solve 
these  problems.  In  many  cases 
the  problem  is  inadequate  nutri¬ 
tion.  Referrals  come  from  friends 
or  relatives  or  even  the  police. 

The  second  program  is  the  day 
center  for  older  adults.  This  is  a 
geriatric  and  psychiatric  treat¬ 
ment  center.  It  operates  five  days 
a  week  at  43rd  Avenue  and 
Noriega  in  the  Sunset  area.  This 
center  works  with  older  persons 
who  are  emotionally  disturbed 
providing  them  with  a  number  of 
services  including  a  socialization 
program. 

The  third  program  is  a  sociali¬ 
zation  and  enrichment  program

"Spring  Thing" 


Ingleside  Presbyterian  Church  was  the  scene  April  28  of  the  festive 
first  annual  .“Spring  Thing”  —  an  impressive  and  varied  display  of 
OMI  talent  brought  together  by  the  OMI-CA  Arts  Committee. 

Music,  dance  and  drama  were  all  represented:  Panamanian  folk 
dancing  by  Leland  and  Grace  Nichols;  folk  music  by  Elaine  Newman 
and  Patty  Pettit  of  the  Blue  Bear  Waltz  School  of  Music;  poetry  ahd 
drama  by  Gil  Chooey ;  and  several  exciting  dances  by  members  of  the 
Community  Dance  Workshop  directed  by  Carolyn  Henderson. 
Well-known  entertainer  Venus  Irving-Prescott  was  a  special  guest. 
The  5  and  6  year  olds  of  the  Ingleside  Cultural  Association’s  Children’s 
Workshop  arrived  late  but  positively  stole  the  show  with  their  dance 
and  individual  recitations. 

An  added  attraction  was  the  art  work  displayed  around  the  room  : 
paintings  by  Diane  Correll,  Suzanne  Jackson  and  Gil  Chooey; 
macrame  by  Marsha  Christian;  and  sculpture  by  Melvin  Stone. 

Bringing  all  this  talent  together  was  the  inspired  idea  of  the  Arts 
Committee  members  Richard  Ward,  III,  Phyllis  Gomez,  Ann  Rieke 
and  Carolyn  Henderson;  it  was  a  memorable  afternoon.
