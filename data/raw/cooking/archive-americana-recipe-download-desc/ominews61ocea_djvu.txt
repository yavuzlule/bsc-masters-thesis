MARKS  5th 
ANNIVERSARY 

May  marks  the  beginning  of  the 
OMI  News’  sixth  year  as  a  pro¬ 
fessionally  printed  newspaper, 
its  forerunner,  the  OMI  News¬ 
letter,  was  launched  in  Septem¬ 
ber  of  1968.  Many  volunteers 
joined  the  monthly  stapling  par¬ 
ties  to  put  the  mimeographed 
sheets  together. 

The  News,  like  the  Newsletter, 
has  been  an  entirely  volunteer 
operation,  with  the  exception  of 
the  professional  printing  of  the 
News  and,  for  this  past  year,  its 
professional  distribution. 

The  OMI  News  has  been 
generously  aided  by  benevolent 
and  patient  printers  (first  Guten¬ 
berg  Press  and  presently  the 
McDougall  Press),  neighborhood 
merchants,  the  OMI  Community 
Association,  and,  this  year,  the 
San  Francisco  Foundation;  and, 
of  course,  without  the  literally 
hundreds  of  volunteers  who  have 
contributed  countless  hours  over 
the  years,  the  NEWS  could  not 
have  survived  at  all.  Our  thanks 
to  our  many  supporters,  past, 
present  and  future! 


Meals  on  Wheels 

Meals  on  Wheels,  Inc.  of  San 
Francisco  will  begin  delivery  of 
prepared  food  to  homebound 
persons  in  the  greater  OMI  area 
on  May  6.  The  OMI  Ecumenical 
Parish  is  sponsoring  this  service 
and  the  meals  will  be  delivered 
Monday  through  Friday  from  the 
Ingleside  Presbyterian  Church 
on  Ocean  Avenue. 

Meals  on  Wheels  will  use 
volunteers  to  deliver  pre-cooked 
and  individually  packaged  food  to 
eligible  residents  in  this  area. 
The  food  consists  of  a  hot  entree 
with  desert  for  the  noon  meal  and 
a  sandwich  with  fruit  for  a  second 
meal.  Either  milk  and  cereal  or 
juice  and  pastry  is  provided  for 
the  breakfast  meal.  The  cost  of 
the  food  for  one  day  is  $1.75.

eligibility  is  that  the  person  be 
homebound.  If  he  or  she  is  unable 
to  get  out  regularly  for  meals  or 
shopping  or  if  for  any  other 
reason  is  unable  to  prepare  meals 
at  home  then  he  or  she  is

Dialogue 

With  Assemblyman  Leo  McCarthy 

Lack  of  safety  in  our  city  streets  has  become  an  alarming  problem 
in  recent  weeks  as  San  Franciscans  are  plagued  by  a  large  number  of 
violent  crimes. 

The  California  Legislature,  in  conjunction  with  the  California 
Council  on  Criminal  Justice,  is  considering  a  number  of  protection 
programs,  and  already  funding  others,  to  improve  security  in  our 
neighborhoods. 

The  programs  which  I  support  are  called  S.O.S.  —  Safety  on  the 
Streets,  and  will  deal  with  security  for  small  neighborhood  businesses 
to  protect  proprietors  of  our  neighborhood  groceries,  usually 
family-run,  from  robbery  and  assault.  Called  B.E.S.T.  —  Business 
Establishment  Security  Tactics  —  the  program  calls  for  security 
analysis  and  consultation  to  be  provided  by  trained  officers  from 
District  stations  in  a  continuing  effort  to  put  more  patrolmen  in  our 
neighborhoods. 

Operation  R.A.M.  Robbery  Abatement  Mobilization  —  is  already 
funded  by  a  $150,000  grant  this  year.  R.A.M.  is  designed  to  cut  down 
the  leading  crimes  against  the  individual  —  robbery  and  purse 
snatching. 

Safety  Assistance  for  Elder  Persons  (S.A.F.E.)  will  allow  Police 
Department  specialists  to  develop  crime  prevention  programs  for  and 
with  senior  citizens  in  all  districts  of  our  city. 

Other  programs  under  consideration  call  for  improved  police 
community  relations,  increased  minority  recruitment,  drug  abate¬ 
ment  programs,  and  would  promote  faster  police  response  to  reports 
of  serious  violence,  and  adopt  speedier  court  procedures  with  less 
inconvenience  to  witnesses  and  lower  cost  to  the  taxpayer. 

Of  course,  a  major  overhaul  of  our  judicial  process  is  long  overdue. 
At  the  same  time  we  support  increased  police  protection  programs,  we 
believe  prison  reform  arid  improved  rehabilitation  programs  of 
convicted  criminals  must  receive  top  priority. 

For  further  information,  please  contact  my  office  at  557-2662.

to  the  people  Board  Briefs 


By  Genevieve  Jefferson,  Secretary  OMI-CA  Board  of  Directors 

The  Board  of  Directors  welcomed  a  new  member  at  its  April  6,  1974 
meeting,  Captain  David  Sholin  of  the  Salvation  Army  on  Broad  Street. 
He  will  serve  with  I.T.  Bookman  and  Scarlett  Gordon  on  the  Youth 
Committee  whose  immediate  effort  will  be  to  coordinate  the  variety  of 
activities  for  youth  now  going  on  in  the  community.  There  is  a  renewed 
interest  in  developing  a  Youth  Center  and  planning  toward  this  is 
underway.  One  possible  place  suggested  was  Farragut  School,  the  site 
of  the  new  Neighborhood  Community  Center. 

The  Crime  Prevention  Committee  headed  by  I.T.  Bookman  and 
Myron  Zimmerman  have  been  working  with  the  Police  Community 
Relations  Executive  Board  to  consider  ways  of  tackling  the  crime 
problems  in  the  area.  The  Board  of  Directors  took  action  to  oppose  the 
granting  of  a  permit  for  a  gun  shop  in  the  area  and  a  letter  to  this  effect 
was  sent  to  the  Chief  of  Police  and  to  the  Permit  Bureau. 

The  OMI-Community  Association  recently  received  an  unsigned 
letter  complaining  about  the  stray  dogs  in  the  area  and  asking  why 
something  isn’t  done  about  this  problem.  The  Community  Association 
is  a  volunteer  organization  and  cannot  take  on  responsibilities  vested 
in  public  agencies.  Persons  having  complaints  of  this  nature  should 
call  the  Humane  Society  Animal  Shelter  at  621-1700  which  agency 
cruises  about  the  city  to  pick  up  stray  dogs.

Dr.  Riles  talks  with  Ms.  Patricia  Diridoni,  Zone  IV  Chairperson,  at  a 
recent  meeting  with  San  Franciscans  concerned  about  our  schools. 

A  group  of  Zone  chairpersons  and  representatives  and  PTA 
members  from  San  Francisco,  including  several  OMI  residents,  went 
to  visit  Dr.  Wilson  Riles  the  State  Superintendent  of  Public 
Instruction,  as  a  follow-up  to  the  enormously  successful  community 
forum  on  education  held  April  10  at  Everett  Junior  High. 

The  San  Franciscans  felt  that  overall  the  meeting  was  positive  and 
constructive.  Dr.  Riles  spent  2  hours  with  the  groqp  rather  than  the  45 
minutes  originally  scheduled.  His  top  aides  attended  the  meeting. 

A  report  on  the  community  forum  was  presented  and  discussed;  Dr. 
Riles  was  also  provided  with  background  material  on  community 
organizations  and  on  the  current  situation  in  the  schools. 

He  and  his  staff  are  committed  to  assisting  the  San  Francisco 
schools,  and  he  is  seeking  input  and  help  from  all  segments  of  the 
community.  The  San  Franciscans  stressed  the  importance  of 
including  community  parents  on  any  “blue  ribbon  committee”  that 
might  be  appointed. 

Although  some  of  the  conversation  was  informal  and  “off  the 
record,”  the  following  points  were  stressed  again  and  again  by  Dr. 
Riles : 

1.  His  office  has  a  total  priority  to  help  the  San  Francisco  Unified 
School  District. 

2.  The  assistance  of  the  community  is  necessary  for  change. 

3.  The  community  will  be  consulted  before  anything  is  done,  and 
nothing  will  be  “put  over”  on  the  community. 

4.  Unless  there  is  a  consensus  for  change,  nothing  will  happen.

There  will  be  follow-up  meetings  for  concerned  citizens  through  the 
Service  Center  for  Public  Education,  the  first  meeting  to  be  held  May  6 
at  12  noon  at  149-9th  Street.  All  interested  citizens  are  invited  to  attend.

By  JOE  MANNON 

In  my  last  article,  I  made  mention  of  the 
Afro-American  Experience  in  Cooking  held  at  the 
Zone  V  and  VI  offices.  From  the  cookbook  they 
handed  out,  I  have  extracted  another  pearl.  For 
some  time  now,  I  have  been  searching  for  a  good! 
recipe  for  Pickled  Pigs  Feet  and  lo,  between  the  covers  of  the 
Arvie-Kincy  publication,  my  search  ended.  Now  you  might  ask  why  I, 
like  Diogenes,  set  about  such  an  unprofitable  task,  if  so,  a  hot  day’s 
indulgence  of  pigs  knuckles  and  cold  beer  has  obviously  not  colored 
your  grey  life  style. 

PICKLED  PIG’S  FEET 


6-8  pigs  feet  (split) 

1  onion  sliced 
1  carrot  sliced 

1  red  pepper  pod  (take  out  of  your  pickling  spices) 

1  bay  leaf 
sprig  of  parsley 
1  tsp.  whole  cloves 
1  tsp.  dry  mustard 

1  tsp.  celery  seed 
'/a  tsp.  salt 

4  peppercorns 

’  i  part  vinegar  to  3  parts  water  to  cover 

Clean  feet  thoroughly,  place  in  pan  and  cover  with  vinegar-water 
mixture.  Add  remaining  ingredients.  Bring  to  a  boil,  cover,  and 
simmer  gently  until  tender  (approximately  3  hours).  Serves  8.  This 
can  be  served  hot  or  cold  and  will  hold  for  a  week  refrigerated  and 
tightly  covered. 

After  thoroughly  gorging  myself,  I  took  the  recipe  one  step  further  to 
induce  family  and  guest  participation.  The  thick  gelatin  which  formed 
during  refrigeration  provided  the  alternative  method  of  serving. 

Remove  all  the  meat  from  the  knuckles,  heat  and  strain  the  gelatin 
broth,  and  proceed  as  follows: 

3  hard  boiled  eggs,  shelled  and  halved 

2  Polish  sausage  (skinned)  chopped  or  cut  in  strips 

%  chicken  livers  (steamed  or  fried)  and  coarsely  chopped 
y>  cup  chopped  parsley 
Pigs  feet  meat  coarsely  sliced 
1  tsp.  capers  (optional) 

Pour  'fa  inch  liquid  gelatin  in  bottom  of  oblong  pan,  sprinkle  small 
amount  of  parsley  over  surface,  place  in  refrigerator  to  set.  Arrange 
remaining  ingredients  loosely  and  artistically  in  pate  fashion,  pour 
remaining  gelatin  over  mixture  to  cover  about  '4  inch.  Refrigerate, 
unmold,  slice  and  serve  on  lettuce  or  water  cress  with  horseradish 
sauce  or  a  green  mayonnaise  sauce. 


Obviously,  your  combinations  are  endless  depending  upon  what  you 
may  have  in  the  refrigerator,  so  have  fun!

Denman  students  at  work  in  the  math  laboratory. 


James  Denman  Junior  High 
School  has  just  submitted  its 
proposal  for  a  second  year  of 
SB-90  funding  to  improve  mathe¬ 
matics  and  reading  achievement. 

For  the  year  1973-74  the  funds 
have  been  used  only  for  7th  grade 
students.  Added  services  due  to 
these  funds  were  five  additional 
paraprofessionals,  Mrs.  Willie 
Hawthorne,  Mrs.  Teresa  Given, 
Mrs.  Ada  Trout,  Miss  Frances 
Sims,  and  Miss  Marilyn  Mutnik. 
Also,  two  very  capable  parent 
liaisons,  Mrs.  Rosalinda  Solor- 
zano  and  Mr.  Henry  Williams 
were  added  to  the  staff. 

Under  the  energetic  leadership 
of  Mrs.  Jean  Bogiages  and  Mr. 
Fred  Adsit,  coordinators  of  the 
S.B.  Program  at  Denman,  two 
very  fine  labs  for  reading  and 
mathematics  were  developed 
with  the  assistance  of  Mrs.  Rubye 
Stevens  and  Mr.  Tom  Powers. 
These  labs  are  used  to  diagnose, 
prescribe,  and  tutor  on  an 
individual  basis  all  7th  grade 
students  who  need  to  raise  their 
standard  of  achievement  in  math 
and  reading.  The  funds  have  also 


been  used  for  more  services  and 
new  and  improved  instructional 
materials,  a  total  of  $74,000  for 
the  entire  program. 

One  of  the  most  important 
services  added  is  that  of  a 
half-year,  half-time  social  work¬ 
er.  Thanks  to  the  persistent 
efforts  of  the  Parent  Advisory 
Group  headed  by  Mrs.  E.  Taylor, 
Mrs.  Hazel  Ellis,  and  Mr.  John 
Rojo,  the  school  was  able  to 
obtain  Mrs.  Scarlet  Gordon  of  the 
OMI  community  who  is  a  proba¬ 
tion  officer  on  leave. 

The  1974-75  funding  will  service 
only  the  8th  grade  in  order  to 
follow  through  with  the  work 
started  with  this  group.  This 
decision  was  made  by  the  Educa¬ 
tional  Planning  Committee  made 
up  of  parents,  teachers,  para¬ 
professionals  and  administrators. 

Parents  and  interested  com¬ 
munity  members  are  welcome  to 
visit  the  labs  at  James  Denman 
Jr.  High  School  to  see  educational 
progress  in  action.  Call  Mrs. 
Carmen  Trasvina,  asst,  princi¬ 
pal,  586-0840.

Relations  Division  for  the  past  three  years.  The  subject  of  his  presen¬ 
tation  was  how  to  best  protect  yourself  from  loss  and  injury  on  the 
streets  of  the  city .  He  emphasized  two  main  points :  1 )  Carry  very  little 
of  value  in  your  purse  and  2)  give  up  the  purse  without  resisting. 

The  first  part  of  the  presentation  was  a  film  entitled,  “Lady 
Beware.”  This  short  movie  dealt  with  safety  in  the  streets,  in  the  home 
and  in  the  car.  The  key  word  was  “SAFE.”  Each  letter  stands  for  a 
word  of  advice.  The  “S”  stands  for  security,  the  “A”  for  avoid,  the 
“F”  for  flee  and  the  “E”  for  engage. 

Each  point  was  covered  in  an  interesting  manner.  Some  important 
points  to  remember  were  to  use  the  peephole  in  the  front  door  before 
opening  it  to  a  stranger.  (The  chain  locks  are  virtually  worthless  for 
keeping  someone  from  breaking  in  once  the  door  is  partially  opened.) 
Be  careful  of  the  information  you  give  out  on  the  telephone, 
particularly  when  you  are  not  positive  of  the  identity  of  the  caller.  At 
night,  when  leaving  a  gathering,  have  someone  walk  you  to  your  car. 
Also  make  sure  the  car  is  empty  before  getting  in. 

Special  emphasis  was  placed  on  situations  where  women  are  forced 
to  walk  city  streets  alone.  The  best  advice  on  walking  dark,  deserted 
streets  at  night  is  —  DON’T  DO  IT.  However,  if  you  have  to,  then  try  to 
stay  in  the  most  lighted  areas.  Don’t  take  short  cuts,  particularly 
poorly  lighted  side  streets  or  alleys.  Walk  near  the  curb  and  away 
from  dark  doorways.  Make  sure  someone  knows  where  you  are  going 
and  when  you  are  expected  to  arrive  at  your  destination.  Walk  facing 
traffic  whenever  possible. 

Don’t  hesitate  to  stop  and  look  behind  you  to  check  to  see  if  you  are 
being  followed.  If  you  are,  then  run !  Try  to  reach  an  open  business.  As 
a  last  resort,  get  into  a  phone  booth  and  block  the  door.  Call  the  police. 
(Naturally,  this  requires  a  dime.  Be  sure  you  have  one  before  you 
start.) 

A  special  word  of  advice  was  given  to  women  who  drive  at  night.  If 
your  car  becomes  disabled  at  night  in  a  lightly  travelled  area,  stay  in 
the  car,  turn  on  the  distress  blinkers,  keep  the  car  locked,  the  windows 
up  and  wait  for  a  patrol  car.  Even  the  most  friendly  looking  stranger 
may  mean  trouble. 

If  you  are  actually  attacked  and  have  to  fight  back,  use  everything 
that  is  available  to  you  —  a  nail  file,  heels  of  shoes,  even  hair  spray 
will  temporarily  blind  an  attacker.  And  be  sure  and  scream  as  loudly 
as  you  can.  ( Incidentally,  the  use  of  MACE  guns  or  tear  gas  devices  is 
illegal.  However,  a  new  device  will  be  coming  on  the  market  soon  that 
releases  a  loud,  shrill  whistle  when  squeezed  hard.) 

After  the  film,  Officer  Bertuccelli  made  a  few  specific  recommen¬ 
dations.  First,  women  should  keep  only  a  small  amount  of  money  and 
no  credit  cards  in  their  purse.  Place  extra  money,  house  keys  and 
credit  cards  in  a  deep  coat  pocket,  an  inner  pocket  if  possible.  (Men 
should  carry  almost  nothing  of  value  in  their  hip  pocket  wallet.  Carry 
real  valuables  in  an  inner-coat  pocket.) 

If  your  purse  contains  only  items  of  no  value,  then  you  will  not 
hesitate  to  give  up  the  purse  to  an  attacker.  Do  not  try  to  resist.  If  you 
see  there  are  four  men  coming  at  you  for  your  purse,  drop  the  purse  on 
the  ground  and  run.  To  avoid  having  your  purse  picked,  keep  your 
hand  over  the  clasp  and  even  turn  the  purse  upside  down  when  in  a 
crowd.  This  makes  the  purse  impossible  to  pick.  Also,  be  especially 
careful  when  leaving  banks  after  cashing  checks. 

Officer  Bertuccelli  had  a  special  word  for  all  of  those  who  have 
wrought  iron  gates  across  the  front  entrance  of  their  houses.  The  gates 
are  very  strong  but  in  almost  every  case  the  lock  on  the  gate  is  weak 
and  can  be  broken  open  with  one  kick.  The  best  protection  for  your 
house  is  to  get  to  know  your  neighbors  well. 

If  your  group  is  interested  in  hearing  from  the  Police  Community 
Relations  Division,  feel  free  to  call  the  Police  Department  for  further 
information.

KIDS  ENJOY  EGG  HUNT 


The  annual  Giant  Easter  Egg  Hunt  at  Oceanview  Playground  was  a 
tremendous  success,  with  about  200  youngsters  beating  the  bushes  to 
find  the  1500  eggs  hidden  throughout  the  playground. 

All  the  playgrounds  in  the  Oceanview  Satellite  —  Merced  Heights, 
Cayuga,  Chalmers,  Junipero  Serra,  Aptos  and  Oceanview  — 
participated  in  the  event,  which  began  with  a  magic  show  for  the  kids ; 
this  kept  them  happily  imprisoned  in  the  auditorium  while  a  volunteer 
crew  hid  the  eggs. 

Fifteen  lucky  ones  found  the  plastic  eggs  which  entitled  them  to  a 
free  record  each,  contributed  by  L  &  L  Records  on  Broad  Street. 

The  event  was  co-sponsored  by  the  Lillian  Chisom  Eastern  Star 
Chapter  87  which  contributed  80  dozen  eggs,  the  George  W.  Wilson 
Masonic  Lodge  101  which  gave  30  dozen  eggs,  and  by  the  OMI-CA. 

Candy  was  provided  by  many  area  merchants:  Barney’s  Coffee 
Shop  on  Ocean  Ave.;  Blum’s  of  S.F.  of  South  San  Francisco; 
Bowerman’s  Pharmacy  in  Lakeside;  Russell  Richards  Drugs  and 
Westwood  Pharmacy,  both  on  Ocean  Ave.;  and  by  Selmi’s  Market, 
Ashton  and  Holloway.  The  Shabazz  Bakery  on  Randolph  contributed 
20  dozen  cookies.  Thanks  to  all! 


BART  RECOMMENDS  BIKE  PATH  FOR  OMI 


By  BILL  FULLER 

On  March  28,  the  BART  Board 
of  Directors  was  presented  with 
the  final  report  on  recommended 
bicycle  routes  in  the  Bay  Area. 
This  project  was  sponsored  in 
part  by  the  East  Bay  Regional 
Park  District  in  cooperation  with 
BART  personnel.  This  report 
recommends  the  construction  of 
a  bicycle  path,  running  generally 
from  San  Francisco  State  Uni¬ 
versity  to  the  Balboa  Park  BART 
station,  a  distance  of  2.8  miles. 

The  purpose  of  this  study  was 
to  recommend  non-auto  ways  of 
connecting  with  BART  stations  — 
bicycles  being  one  of  the  major 
possibilities.  The  report  also- 
recommended  further  study  by 
BART  of  possible  ways  to  trans¬ 
port  bicycles  on  BART  trains. 
Besides  providing  means  of 
reaching  BART  stations,  the 
report  also  recommended  that 
bicycle  and  pedestrian  routes  be 
provided  from  BART  stations  to 
adjoining  major  park  areas. 

The  San  Francisco  State  Uni¬ 
versity  to  Balboa  Park  station 
bicycle  route  is  one  of  six  demon¬ 
stration  projects  recommended, 
but  the  only  one  suggested  for 
San  Francisco.  The  others  are  to 
be  located  in  the  East  Bay.  The 
suggested  route  is  the  same  as 
the  one  reported  in  the  prelimi¬ 
nary  report  published  in  Novem¬ 
ber,  1973.  The  route  would 
actually  start  at  Font  Blvd.  and 
Lake  Merced  Blvd.  It  would 
proceed  down  Font  to  Holloway 
and  East  on  Holloway  to  Harold 
Street,  North  on  Harold  one  block 
to  Ocean  Avenue  and  across 
Ocean  Avenue.  It  would  continue 
East  on  the  North  side  of  Ocean  to 
the  Balboa  Park  station.  From 
there  the  proposed  route  would 
proceed  East  on  Ocean  to  Persia 
and  on  into  McLaren  Park.  This 
route  would  then  tie  together 


Lake  Merced,  San  Francisco 
State  University,  City  College  of 
San  Francisco,  Balboa  Park 
BART  station  and  McLaren 
Park. 

BART  envisions  separate  bi¬ 
cycle  lanes  as  a  minimum 
standard  and  strongly  recom¬ 
mends  the  construction  of  a 
barricade  between  auto  and 
bicycle  traffic.  This  route  does 
involve  complications,  including 
crossings  at  Ocean  Avenue,  juni¬ 
pero  Serra  Blvd.  and  19th  Ave¬ 
nue,  as  well  as  problems  with  the 
street  width  of  Holloway. 

BART  has  advised,  however, 
that  they  have  no  funds  for  the 
construction  of  these  routes  and
