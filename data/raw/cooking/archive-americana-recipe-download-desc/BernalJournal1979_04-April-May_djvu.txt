BHCF Buys 
First House 

by Buck Bagot 

The Bernai Heights Community Foundation 
(BHCF) has arranged to acquire its first lot and 
house on the Hill. Located at 18 Bonview 
Street, the acquisitions will result in reasona- 
bly priced, attractive homes for Bernai Heights 
residents. A new home will be designed and 
constructed on the lot by the BHCF. The 
house will be rehabilitated with low-interest 
State loan money. Both will be made available 
to low and moderate income Bernai Heights 
residents at lower than market level mortgage 
rates. . 

The BHCF, and its community development 
program, were mandated in the Elsie St. Plan, 
passed by the neighborhood and the SF City 
Planning Commission in Spring, 1978. The 
BHCF applied for Community Development 
Block Grant funding, and was included in the 
1979 budget. It received $150,000 for acquisi- 
tion. Although the funds will not become avail- 
able until June, when the deal will be closed, 
an agreement has been reached with the Mis- 
sion Housing Development Corp. for funds for 
a binder and a down payment. 

Bernai Heights has 250-350 open, buildable 
lots. A number of the block clubs, and the 
BHCF, feel that it is imperative that a large 
number of these lots be developed with hous- 
ing most residents can afford. If left to the 
speculators, the influence of large numbers of 
box-like, expensive houses will be felt by ren- 
ters and home-owners alike. In the next few 
months, the BHCF, in cooperation with the 
Northwest Bernai Block Club, will be contact- 
ing lot owners in the area bounded by Coso, 
Cortland, Bonview, and Mission. It will seek to 
purchase lots, arid any existing housing up for 
sale. 

The BHCF is a non-profit community 
development corp. Any housing will be made 
available to Bernai residents- who qualify 
through a lottery. It will be some time before 
housing will be ready for occupancy by its 
eventual owners.

BERNAL BULLDOG LEADS THE GOOD LIFE. 



by, Joanne Molloy 

If ugliness had four legs and a tail, it would be 
a bulldog. Or so the untrained observer might 
say. To others— the self-proclaimed "bulldog 
persons" — a bulldog is the perfect result of 
centuries of cross-breeding. Devoted owners 
ride a circuit of bulldog shows in the state to 
show off their mug-faced pedigrees. And when 
their champion finally gets that blue ribbon, its 
pups are worth $650 a piece. 

Bulldogs were first bred in England for the 
sport of "bullbaiting," a form of meat tenderiz- 
ing while the bull was still alive. When public 
pressure made bullbaiting illegal in 1835, the 
British leisure class turned the bulldogs on 
one another for sport in pits made for the pur- 
pose. By selective breeding of Mastiffs and 
terriers, owners actually c^esigned a dog with 
the pit-fight in mind. Their champion bulldog 
had loose flaps of skin from chin to chest, with 
a receding head and snout. If attacked here, 
the dog's throat would be protected, enabling 
it to breathe. The bulldog's hefty torso is 
packed with strength, enabling it to leap eight, 
feet into the air and knock. down a large adult. 

Jeanne Hamer's bulldog "Buster" is just 
such a champion. His lineage papers, going 
back five generations on both sides, give him 
deeper roots than most Americaris. Buster 
does not engage in pit-fighting: the toughest 
he gets is chasing a speeding VW up Elsie 
Street. No, Buster's blue ribbons are for looks 
alone. When Buster parades around thie show' 
ring, specialized bulldog judges know.he's got 
what it takes: the swaggering walk, curlie-cue 
tail, and jowls that would put Howard Jarvis to 
shame. 



A bulldog show is not unlike the Miss Ameri- 
ca pageant in its emphasis on beauty over 
talent. Buster and his canine companions are 
not required to do any tricks: they can't. As ex- 
pert Enno Meyer says in his book. The Bulldog, 
by breeding the bulldog for its somewhat 
squashed head, 19th century breeders made 
their brains, "smaller than in any other race (of 
dog) . . . making it inferior to all others in 
everything related to intelligence."

vantage at the other end — he can't reach his 
own tail to scratch it. Any human assistance 
given to him in this area is greatly appreciated.

We were somewhat surprised at Phil 
Johnson's article in the February — March 
issue. Mr. Johnson's views of why Proposition 
U lost make very little sense, especially in the 
light of the $500,000 advertising campaign that 
the real estate interests waged to distort the 
truth. Mr. Johnson gives three reasons why 
Prop. U lost. First, that it only addressed a 
small part of the problem, not the underlying 
causes for high rents. That's true, of course, 
but Prop. U was never designed to solve the 
overall problem. It was simply a one year 
measure Intended to pass on property tax sav- 
ings from Prop. 13 to the renters who pay 
those taxes, a fact that was never addressed In 
the landlords media blitz. 
. Next Mr. Johnson clairhs that many build- 
ings didn't receive property tax savings. While 
it is true that many "speculated" properties 
didn't get much back because of inflated 
values, the vast majority of residential property 
did get tax relief, an average savings of over 
50% according to the City's budget analyst in 
an August 1978 report to the board of super- 
visors. Last, Mr. Johnson says Prop U lost 
because it ignored the Impact of the sewer tax, 
which is also being passed on to renters. 
Again, Prop. U was a property tax relief 
measure, aimed at putting property tax relief 
dollars into the pockets of renters where they 
belong. It had nothing to do with the s6wer tax. 

There was nothing "unfair" about Proposi- 
tion U. It would have simply passed on to ren- 
ters the same tax relief that homeowners got 
from Prop. 13. Mr. Johnson says that landlords 
are doing this on a voluntary basis, but that 
just isn't so, as any renter can tell you; 

Finairy, Mr. Johnson asks to go biegging to 
the state government for rent relief. You'll all 
remember how much Jerry Brown and the 
state legislature "have done for renters the next 
time the landlord raises the rent. Strong local 
organizing, not appeals to Sacramento, is the 
key to renter relief. 

Mr. johnson's article itself is a perfect exam- 
ple of how confused a person/ voter can get 
when hit with half a million dollars In lies and 
distortions.

Jane Fonda: "Life of the movie." 

China Syndrome 

by Eric Craven 
I often attend movies about some "cause " 
with fear and dread. I may agree with the 
message, but the acting, plot, music, cutting 
and everything else make me so embarrassed, 
it reminds me of my grade-school plays. I wish 
I could quietly slide under my seat. 
Not so the China Syndrome. 
Good story. Well-acted. A must-see. Espe- 
cially In light of the recent Three Mile Island 
nuclear disaster, the worst in U.S. history. 

Actually, China Syndrome Is not about an ac- 
cident occurring, but about a near-accident. 
Everything turns out all right. In a way, that's 
what lends it credibility. If It had been about a 
full blown nuclear disaster, it would have been 
almost too predictable. Having Jane Fonda in 
it— who has been cast by the media as an 
unacceptable radical — I would expect It to 
portray the worst situation. ^ 

But it's not the worst. It's hot Three Mile Is- 
land. The story pulled me In and carried me 
along toward disaster, but at the last moment It 
pulled me back. 

Jack Lemmon does a very good job — not 
the somewhat confused, curled-lip fall guy of 
some second rate comedy. No, he's a nuclear 
engineer who grows as the problems he finds 
force him on to the next step. 

Mike Douglas is firey and difficult. He cons- 
tantly pushes from step to step. 

And Jane Fonda, starting as a somewhat 
befuddled, plastic newscaster, gets more and 
more Involved In the news Itself. She is the life 
of the movie. 

So, without telling the entire story, what 
more can I say . . . except, try to see it.

A MURAL AT DIAMONT TOOL & DIE SHOWS HOW STEEL IS MADE. 



The tool and die man with a vision 



by Michael Kazin 

As soon as you walk into Setly Diamant's 
tool arvd die company m \h& warehouse dis- 
trict along Afmy Street, something seems 
unusual. The floors and machinery are im- 
maculate; the lighting doesn't strain the eye. 
On the back wall sits a mural depicting the 
process of making steel in the deep, earthen 
colors of ore-laden mountains and hulking 
mills. Strangest of all, the three young ma- 
chinists appear to be fascinated by their work. 
They come to Dramaht with questions and 
suggestions about this measurement, or that 
precision cut and then go back to the job with 
a passion that is absent from the majority of in- 
dustrial jobs, which are repetitive and require 
little skill. 

The workers at Diamant's shop are appren- 
tices engaged in a unique program which 
pays them a good wage while they learn. Their 
teacher is also their boss. Diamant, a Danish- 
born machinist, believes fervently in the tradi- 
tional method of training apprentices in the 
skilled trades. "The onjy way to teach a-guy a 
trade is by putting him to work," he says. 

Diamant describes the woeful situation for 
would-be apprentices. "The unions don't want 
to increase the number of journeymen appren- 
tices. "The unions don't want to increase the 
number of journeymen in the trades and the 
big companies depend on the schools." When 
he attempted to get CETA money from the 
federal government last year, Diamant got 
caught in a blizzard of red tape. "There are so 
many laws protecting them (apprentices) that 
no-one will hire them." 



So the energetic craftsman is doing the task 
himself. Each year since he opened the busi- 
ness in 1975, Diamant has hired one young ap- 
prentice. Working with a professor at the 
O'Connell Technical School in the Mission, 
the trainees get a full combination of 
classroom theory and work experience. After 
three years in the shop, they are ready for a 
career in the skill of constructing complex 
molds and machine tools for manufacturing 
plants. According to Diamant, good tool-and- 
die makers have become a rare breed in Amer- 
ican industry. 

Selly Diamant is different from most bosses 
in one other way: he doesn't want his business 
to get any bigger. "We get most of our orders 
from the factories around here; it's kind of a 
neighborhood concern." The mural — done 
last December by a friend named Selma 
Brown— is a symbol of Diamant's vision. In the 
center of it, five workers of different races 
bend to their tasks with strength and confi- 
dence.

CHARTER CHAT 



San Francisco's Charter Commission is' 
gearing up for the massive job of rewriting the 
document which structures and defines City 
government. To be completed by August, 
1980, this new "constitution" will be submitted 
to the voters whose lives it will govern. Four 
Commissioners up-dated the District Nine 
Community Council^ on the Charter reform 
process. 

Chairman Wilson Chang outlined the five 
sections to be reviewed: The structure of 
government; its legislative powers; and its 
dealings with Federal and State funding agen- 
cies. 

Commissioner Pat Jackson reminded ths 
Council that San Franciscans have vote j 
twice for Chiarter reform, "They want a ne v 
Charter." She hopes the Commission cai 
reach a consensus which the voters will ac - 
cept. Chang said implementation may come i t 
stages. 

All the Commissioners agreed the Charter is 
"out-of-date,;' "inefficient," too cumbersome 
and too full of amendments. 

Commissionei', Jim Haas,, a Bernal resident, 
said "I took on the Charter as my special pro-, 
ject," his father was on the Commission which 
drew up the present Charter in 1931 . Haas said 
he plans to listen "to everyone, from the 
owners of the Chronicle to the people on my 
block." 

Commissioner Tom Cahill said, "I would like 
... to get in-put from every segment and sec- 
tion of the City." He urged citizens groups "to 
hammer out" position papers for the Commis- 
sion. Cahill, Police Chief from 1958 to '70, pre- 
dicted this may be the last chance for Charter 
reform "for a long tim^." 

The District Nine Council voted to form its 
own Charter Reform Committee, to endorse 
passage of the Commission's budget, and to 
urge District residents to attend the Charter 
Meetings on the Second Tuesday and Last 
Thursday of each month, at 7:00 P.M. In the 
State Building at Civic Center.

Natural History 

by Barbara M. Pitschel 

Spring has, at last, come to San Francisco. 
Our hill is at its most beautiful, covered with 
wiidflowers. (We are now reaping the rewards 
of having waited for an unusually late spring: 
the January, February, March and April 
flowers are all blooming at -the same time, a 
spectacular sights) 

This month I shall introduce you to my 
favorite Bernal wildflower, the Sun Cup 
(Oenothera ovata), whose yellow flowers 
emerge in clusters (each leaf-axil producing 
one flower), from a basal rosette of oval (some- 
times slightly wavy-edged) leaves. An alter- 
nate common name. Golden Eggs, Is derived 
from the egg-like shape and nest-like quality 
of the leaves. Each flower has four petals 
(each 1/2" to 3/4" long), four sepals, and eight 
stamens. 

Sun Cups are in the Evening Primrose 
Family (ONA'GRACEAE), members of which 
are easily recognizable by several fairly dis- 
tinct characteristics. They close their petals in 
the absence of sun (evenings or cloudy 
weather). Their flower parts are always pre- 
'sent in fours (and eights: the stamens), includ- 
ing four-celled inferior ovaries. (This term is in 
no way to be construed as sexist, meaning 
simply that the seed-producing organ of the 
plant is below, rather than above, the surface 
of the flower.) In the case of the Sun Cup this 
trait is so exaggerated that the seeds are pro- 
duced just beneath the ground* The flowers 
actually have no stems at all. The flowers that 
lodk like cups are, in fact, more like cham- 
pagne glasses. 




The Sun Cup 



Although this appears to be an excellent 
year for this species, it cannot be considered 
common on our hill. (In some years I have 
found no plants, in others only a few.) I have 
seen them growing only on the south slope, in 
the area around and above the Ellsworth 
Street steps. Because the seeds are produced 
underground, dispersal would appear to be 
limited-, causing plants to appear year after 
year in the same places. It Is possible that 
some seed dispersal could be attributed to 
burrowing seed-eaters (gophers?). It is also 
possible that surface disturbance could un- 
cover the shallowly-buried seeds, slightly in- 
creasing the chances of wider distribution.

Pea nut Butter Cookies 



by Lil Kruse 

I was very fond of my Mother-in-law and her 
peanut butter cookies. I will share her recipe. 

Preheat oven to 350°. Soften 2 cubes butter^ 
add one cup brown sugar, firnnly packed, and 
1 cup of white sugar, blend together. Add 1 
cup peanut butter, 2 eggs and 2 teaspoons 
baking soda, blend. Gradually add 2-1/2 cups 
flour and a little salt. Drop by teaspoon onto 
cookie sheet and pat with fork, moistened in 
water. 

Bake 12 to 15 minutes or until lightly gold 
and crisp. 
Eat and enjoy.

President's Corner 



by Rex Coultas 



WHAT I HAVE SEEN 

When I first started working on the Bernal 
Journal several years ago, it was still con- 
sidered a newsletter of the Bernal Heights 
Association (BHA). Last year before I became 
president of the BHA, the editors of the Bernal 
Journal decided unilaterally to dissolve this 
relationship and make the Bernal Journal an 
independent newspaper. 1 voted against this 
decision, but to ho avail. It was my feeling that, 
without a voice, the BHA would die. 
Historically the Bernal Journal has been a part 
of the BHA, the By-Laws of the association 
provide for such a newspaper to be called 
"The Bernal Journal," and the association has 
at various times^provided financial support for 
running the paper as well. I was also con- 
cerned that provisions should be made for he 
orderly, democratic transfer of authority from 
one editorship to another, so that the news- 
paper would continue to truly be a "forum of 
the community" and not fall prey to exploita- 
tion by private interest groups for profit or 
political ambition. 

WHAT IS NOW 

Unfortunately such provisions do not now 
exist under the current setup, nor are any 
planned for the future as fair as I know. 

When I accepted the job of president of the 
BHA, my two concerns were (1 ) to see the BHA 
continue and not go inactive, as no one else 
was willing at that time to take the job (so far 
we have been successful in this endeavor), 
and (2) to try to keep the Bernal Journal and the 



Bernal Heights Association together so that 
collectively they might continue making an im- 
portant contribution to the community (in this 
endeavor we have not been so successful). 

WHAT WILL BE HEREAFTER? 

It will soon be time for election of new 
officers for the BHA. Will anyone step forward 
and-volunteer for the jobs, or will the organiza- 
tion flounder? We have been operating with a 
skeleton crew this year, just a president and a 
treasurer, as no one else would volunteer for 
the other jobs. 

What is the direction the community is tak- 
ing? Will there be a continuing proliferation of 
special interest groups, block clubs, SAFE, 
Commqnity Boards, etc., splintering up the po- 
tential cohesiveness of the neighborhood, or 
shall the members of the community support a 
strong, broad-spectrum neighborhood 
association whose town hall function can 
bring out a mayor or a supervisor and be heard 
with respect in. City Hall? 

These questions need to be answered with 
action, not words. .That watchword of our 
Republic is enough already: "United we stand, 
divided we fall*" ' .

become a member of the BHA, please use the

nal Journal. Ournext meeting is scheduled for
